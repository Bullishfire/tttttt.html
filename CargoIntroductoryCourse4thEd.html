The History of Air Cargo

The transportation of cargo by air began in 1910. For the first time in history, 10 bales of silk were transported in the United States from Huffman Prairie Flying Fields in Dayton, Ohio to Columbus, Ohio. It was only a 100 mile journey, but that was the foundation of the multi-billion dollar air cargo industry as we know it today.

Europe followed the American innovation and in August 1911, the newspaper Berliner Morgenpost was the first European air cargo consignment to be shipped from Berlin-Johannisthal to Frankfurt on the Oder.

Regular airmail service was started between Washington D.C. and New York City in 1918. Along with airmail service, the regular shipping of commodities by air began in the 1920's. In 1925, the Ford Motor Company successfully started shipping freight, using Henry Ford's Express Company.

The air cargo business expanded in the 1930's as larger airliners came into service, but air cargo still lagged far behind passengers and mail business.

Still, for certain categories of compact, lightweight and high-value items, air transport proved highly useful. Typical air cargo in those days included jewelry, cut flowers, high-fashion clothing, movie reels, pharmaceuticals, and high priority replacement and spare parts for machinery. Even some live animals were carried.

After World War II, when larger, more efficient planes became available, airlines increased their cargo operations and several all-cargo companies were formed. At this point, almost anything that would fit inside the fuselage of an aircraft was transported by air. During the Berlin airlift (1948–49), military aircraft carried not only food, but also tons of coal and construction equipment. All kinds of livestock traveled in pressurized cargo planes. Perishables, frozen foods, heavy machinery, automobile parts, and even complete cars were also characteristic of air cargo. Smaller shipments were usually packed in large containers, to facilitate easy handling and to cut down on pilferage.

The 1970's saw a breakthrough for integrated carriers (Integrators), which offered an overnight door-to-door delivery of small packages and letters. These Integrators were now competing with national postal services. In its early years, this type of freight grew by more than 17% per year.

Today, two main factors define the choice of air transport for certain products: the value of the commodities and the speed with which they have to be transported. Urgent materials such as serums and human donor organs, spare parts, documents, samples, hazardous goods and high-value goods such as diamonds, gold, electronic equipment are common air cargo products. Air transport is ideal for certain food products, like salmon and lobster from Ireland or Canada, lamb from Australia, exotic fruit, and plants and flowers. Also expensive cars or cars destined for areas that are difficult to reach (e.g. Africa) are carried by aircraft.The last decade has seen a growth in the demand for air transport of pharmaceutical goods.

The last two decades have seen shippers and consignees expecting new added value services from carriers and freight forwarders, as well as higher visibility of the status of their shipments. The growth in demand for the transport of pharmaceutical and healthcare industry products has resulted in the introduction of a Temperature Control Regulations Manual for the proper handling of time and temperature sensitive shipments. Technology is enabling carriers and forwarders to push shipment status out to interested parties. It is also the key to the transformative goal to eliminate all paper documents from the transportation chain.

Course Introduction

The IATA International Cargo Agents Training Program was launched in 1977 to meet the growing demand for well-trained staff in air cargo agencies.

The course material is produced by a team of cargo training experts from the IATA Training and IATA Member Airlines and is revised regularly. These cargo experts, also produce the examinations. IATA Training does the overall administration of the IATA Cargo Agents Training Program.

The IATA Diploma guarantees a high standard of training and has gained worldwide recognition and acceptance, as a quality product, by the world's airlines and air cargo forwarders' associations. It is a unique qualification for anyone wishing to make a career in the air cargo business.

The national administration of the program is handled by IATA Local Coordinators who work for the national IATA Member Airline, the IATA Billing and Settlement Plan (BSP), the Cargo Accounts Settlement System (CASS), IATA Financial and Distribution Services (FDS), or the national Cargo Agents' Association.

4 Cargo Introductory Course

Course Objectives

Cargo Introductory Course Textbook

During this course you will gain a broad understanding of the air cargo agency, airline operations and the role of a Freight Forwarder. You will also become familiar with related subjects such as industry terminology and abbreviations, world geography, air cargo acceptance, basic rating principles and the Air Waybill.

After successful completion of the course, you will be able to do the following:

• Offer appropriate and professional advice to clients concerning air freight shipments in accordance with the IATA rules and procedures;

• Make appropriate arrangements and reservations for air cargo shipments in accordance with the IATA rules and procedures;

• Correctly apply published rates and charges for air cargo shipments in accordance with the applicable IATA resolutions;

• Complete air waybills accurately and in accordance with the applicable IATA resolutions;

• Prepare shipments ‘ready for carriage’ in accordance with the applicable IATA resolutions;

• Understand the e-Freight initiatives and e-AWB process.

Students who pass the examination successfully will receive an IATA Diploma which is recognized all over the world.

For Whom is the IATA Cargo Introductory Course

Intended?

This course has been designed primarily for the operations staff of international

air cargo agents. However, it is also equally useful to:

• All sales and marketing staff of cargo agents and air freight forwarders

(consolidators);

• The sales, reservations, and acceptance staff of airlines, airport handling,

and surface transportation companies;

• Shipping department staff of manufacturers, wholesalers, importers, and

exporters; and

• Junior and middle management staff of cargo agents, airlines, and

manufacturers who wish to expand or refresh their knowledge of current

international air cargo rating procedures.

As the course is updated regularly, it will also prove useful to persons already

working in the industry who wish to keep their knowledge of air cargo

acceptance and rating procedures up-to-date.

Persons holding the IATA Cargo Introductory Diploma are considered qualified

under the terms of the applicable IATA resolutions and will have a strong

competitive advantage when applying to work for an IATA Cargo Agency.

Recommended Pre-requisite: A good understanding of written English, world

geography, and basic mathematics are essential. IATA Cargo English Course

is a good base for this training.

The material and examinations for this course are published by IATA in English

and Spanish Languages.

This Module enables you to understand the difference between an IATA cargo agent and a consolidator, their respective functions and the relationship between the air cargo agent and his customers, on one hand, and the airline on the other.

Module 3—World Geography

This Module consists of the following Units:

• Geography and IATA 3-letter codes;

• Time differences;

• Calculation of transportation time.

This Module will introduce students to world geography and time differences. The review exercise of this Module requires you to identify countries and cities as well as major international airports.

When marking countries, cities (with codes) and airports (with codes), you should work from memory.

Step One: Read the list of countries, cities and airports at the beginning of each section while simultaneously locating them on a world map.

Step Two: Put the world map and the list aside, and indicate as many countries, cities and airports as you can remember, together with their 3-letter codes.

Step Three: Take the world map and the list of countries, cities and

You can expand your expertise by consulting a complete edition of The Air

After studying this Module you will be able to name and locate countries and

Module 4—Use of Guides

This Module provides a survey of the guides used in air cargo transportation

and it describes its structure and presentation.

The instruction in this Module is based on the OAG (formerly known as the

Official Airline Guide) Air Cargo Guide and OAG Worldwide Flight Guide.

Extracts of these guides are included in the textbook.

When completing the exercises in this Module, only use the extracts that are

included in the course materials as the answers are based on them. In real-life

situations the current issues of these guides should be referred to.

You will need to apply the information and instructions in this Module in order to

gain a reasonable degree of speed and accuracy in reading schedules and

selecting suitable routings for shipments.

The subject matter of this Module and Modules 5 & 6 are essential for Module 7

“Air Cargo Acceptance” in which the subjects of all three Modules will be

applied to specific areas.

Upon completion of this Module, you will be able to use the guides and

construct unpublished transfer connections for air cargo shipments.

Module 5—Aircraft

This Module describes some of the physical characteristics of the current

aircraft types used in commercial transportation of cargo and the effects of

these characteristics on the acceptance of cargo.

Certain restrictions imposed on shipments because of their weight and

dimensions are explained (with illustrations) in this Module. You will be

introduced to the use of loading charts and Carriers' regulations that help in

deciding whether a given shipment can be loaded into a given aircraft, or not.

Therefore you must refer to the relevant sections of the TACT (The Air Cargo

Tariff) Rules. You must always consult the concerned Carrier(s) in case of any

doubt.

The best additional training for you is to see several types of aircraft in

operation at the nearest airport and to watch loads being assembled or loaded

onto pallets or into containers. You will gain an excellent grasp of the practical

problems involved and develop awareness of the dimensions which fit the

various aircraft.

After completing this Module, you will be able to describe aircraft character

istics; use the loading charts reproduced in Section 8.2 of the TACT Rules and

list the different types of Unit Load Devices (ULD) along with their character

istics.

Module 6—Handling Facilities

This Module describes the range of facilities normally available for the handling of cargo at major airports including airport and carriers' equipment available for the transportation of air cargo. The exercises cover customs clearance, storage

and loading/unloading facilities and various equipment available at different airports.

At the end of this Module you will be able to list the most common facilities for aircraft handling and you will be able to use the TACT Rules, Section 7.3 to find out if those are available at major airports worldwide.

Module 7—Air Cargo Acceptance

This Module emphasizes the various factors which must be taken into consideration when accepting a shipment for carriage by air.

It covers:

• Instructions for carriage;

• Acceptance based on the Shipper's Letter of Instruction; • Acceptance of special cargoes;

• Supporting documentation requirements.

This Module is based on the information you have acquired in the three preceding Modules. A thorough knowledge of air cargo guides and airline schedules, of aircraft capacity as well as restrictions, handling facilities and requirements is essential to establish whether or not a shipment described in a “Shipper's Letter of Instructions” can be accepted.

This subject requires a systematic approach, great attention to detail and much practice.

Ensuring that a shipment is “ready for carriage” is one of the main tasks of a cargo agent. Your ability to do so will be thoroughly tested in the final examination.

After completing this Module, you will be able to decide whether or not a shipment can be accepted for carriage by air in accordance with IATA's rules and procedures.

Module 8—Cargo Booking Procedures

This Module introduces you to the airline booking systems and procedures. It tells you why, how and when a booking should or must be made. The concept of carrier specific “Cargo Products” is introduced.

At the end of this Module, you will be able to make cargo bookings with airlines in accordance with the applicable IATA rules and procedures.

Module 9—Cargo Automation

After going through this Module, you will be able to list the main functions of SITA and to give a general description of Cargo-IMP, Cargo-XML, EDI, UN/EDIFACT, CCS, CASS, CARGO iQ and e-freight.

Module 10—Air Cargo Rates and Charges

This Module covers:

• The Air Cargo Tariff (TACT);

• The chargeable weight;

• Rounding off rules for dimensions, weight, and currencies;

• General rules;

• Precedence of rates and charges;

• Valuation charges;

• Charges collect shipments;

• Disbursements;

• Other charges and fees.

In Module 10 you will learn how to apply published through (direct) rates to

normal consignments, based on the use of TACT Rules and Rates. An

abridged training edition of these TACT publications (selected number of cities)

Module 10 does not deal with rate constructions and combinations as these are

beyond the scope of this course. They are explained in the Cargo Advanced

answers with those in the answer sheets at the end of this course guide.

Speed and accuracy in calculating the appropriate transportation charges of a

consignment comes with practice. For additional training, you should select the

applicable rates and calculate the charges of shipments as often as possible.

When you have finished this chapter you will be able to calculate and rate a

shipment based on the IATA resolutions for cargo shipments as published in

The Air Cargo Tariff (TACT).

Study Tips

You are about to begin the study of a very important course, especially if your daily work is directly concerned with the processing of national and inter national shipments of various goods by air.

This course will provide you with an in-depth knowledge of the subject. It also contains practical exercises designed to familiarize you with the different rules applicable to the air transport.

The following recommendations will help you to make the best use of the training materials:

1. Study all subjects in the sequence presented in the manual.

2. Read the explanatory notes carefully before working through the examples and make reference to the appropriate training tool (TACT) for a particular route or table.

3. Work through the illustrative examples in a logical sequence. Do not miss any recommended steps and, if you have any doubt, refer again to the explanatory note or appropriate rule.

4. Complete all exercises. Work through each exercise while referring to the training materials and the TACT training editions as necessary. Then compare your answers with the answers contained in the answer pages at the end each Module of the training book. If your answers are incorrect, work through the exercise once more whilst referring to the training materials to find out where you might have gone wrong. Study the entire Module and do the exercise(s) again, as needed.

5. The exercises are intended to create a change of pace in the course of study. They also allow students to ensure that they have understood that part of the Module for which the exercise applies.

6. After comparing answers with those given in the answer sheets, the student should determine the areas where errors were made. These subjects should then be reviewed and a further attempt made (allowing enough time to elapse between first and second attempts so that the answers cannot be memorized).

This training manual has been compiled for worldwide application and consequently only the most important rules and exceptions have been covered. However, it must be realized that there are numerous additional exceptions that may apply on a local or regional basis.

A standard format has been used in all examples contained in this course. Please use the same format in your calculations as it will remind you of the correct step-by-step procedure to be followed in each case. As a general rule, write down the applicable rates before you begin to choose the final rate. A neat presentation will be a great help for us in correcting and even more important it will prevent you from making errors!

The International Civil Aviation Organization (ICAO) was founded by the

• Economic Development of Air Transport–Foster the development of a

• Environmental Protection–Minimize the adverse environmental effects of

civil aviation activities.

1.2.3.2 Cargo Accounts Settlement Systems–CASS

Cargo Account Settlement Systems (CASS) is designed to simplify the billing

and settling of accounts between airlines and freight forwarders. It operates

through CASSlink, an advanced, global, web-enabled e-billing solution.

CASS is operating in about 100 countries, serving over 500 airlines, general

sales and service agents (GSSAs) and ground handling companies.

To ensure complete compatibility with the marketplace, the systems are

introduced in close cooperation with national associations of cargo agents and

freight forwarders. Under the CASS domain are two independent systems:

CASS-Export and CASS-Import and Terminal Charges (CASS-ITC) which can

be implemented either separately or together on a country by country basis.

CASS is open to IATA and non-IATA air carriers as well as General Sales and

Service Agents (GSSA) and Ground Handling Agents (GHA) that satisfy the

CASS qualifying criteria.

Advantages of CASS-Export

The introduction of CASS significantly reduces expensive and time-consuming clerical functions through central reporting, billing and remittance management. The benefits that CASS-Export generates include:

• Application of current technology justified by economies of scale;

• Standardization of electronic data processing (EDP) procedures and forms;

• Production of industry standard invoices by the settlement office;

• Simplification of remittance: Agents pay one amount to CASS covering payment to all carriers. Carriers receive one amount covering payment from all agents;

• Elimination of duplication of data entry. Agents and Carriers using their own systems may deliver or receive sales and standard invoice information in electronically readable form;

• Centralization of billing error resolution (by the settlement office);

• Handling of remittance irregularities conducted impartially, using standard rules, without discrimination;

• Enhancement of financial control and improvement of cash flow; • Provision of reliable and up-to-date sales statistics;

• Supervision of processing by an IATA employed Manager; • AWB Stock Management using CASSLink;

• No fees to IATA Cargo Agents for participation in CASS.

Principal Function of CASS–Export

Carriers participating in a CASS, capture their air waybill (AWB) data in their own systems and then upload that data to the CASS system. From that data, the CASS produces standard invoices. A Carrier may elect to receive a file containing electronic records of each AWB that has been recorded in the CASS sytem.

During the processing of air waybill data, the CASS advises the ISS (IATA Settlement System) Manager of any discrepancies in reporting or remittance by Carriers or Agents.

Each Agent receives an invoice in respect of each Carrier they work with, and a statement indicating the total amount due to all Carriers. Each Carrier receives the reciprocal accounting information. Agents have the option to ask that invoices be produced and sent to a centralized location (by country) or to each branch location.

Both Agents and Carriers have adequate opportunity to query an unclear entry, as the invoices are produced about two weeks in advance of the remittance date (exact production schedules vary by CASS). On that date, the Agent remits the total amount to the CASS's hinge account, from which one payment is made to each Carrier.

The Agency Administrator will carry out appropriate follow-up action as set out in the Cargo Agency Rules. In case of a dispute, parties may ask the ISS Manager to act as a neutral mediator between them.

Advantages of CASS–Import and Terminal Charges

The principle of CASS-ITC is similar to that of CASS-Export. Intermediaries

contracted by and acting on behalf of consignees handle most import air cargo.

Under CASS-ITC, an intermediary that meets the specified criteria may

execute an agreement with participating Carriers to become a Recipient.

Recipients benefit from standard credit facilities in accordance with the

CASS-ITC procedures. There is an established billing and remittance

schedule.

The Operation of CASS–Import and Terminal Charges

On arrival of a consignment, the Delivering Company notifies the consignee

(the importer). In most cases the importer will ask a Recipient to arrange that

the cargo be delivered to his address. The Recipient, upon authentication of

the appropriate form, accepts the cargo and the responsibility for any import

charges. An Import Billing Instruction is sent to the CASS office for processing.

Similar to CASS-Export, the CASS office prepares invoices that are sent to the

Recipient in respect of each CASS Delivering Company, together with a

summary indicating the total amount due to all CASS Delivering Companies.

Each Delivering Company receives the reciprocal accounting information. The

Recipient remits the total amount to the CASS office's hinge account from

which monies owed to CASS Delivering Companies are settled.

CASS Implementation/Operation

A study is conducted by IATA to ascertain the feasibility of implementing a

CASS in a given country. The proposal to introduce a CASS for a given

country/area may be made by the Head Office of any Member, or group of

Members or an Agents' Association. Proposals are to be addressed to the FDS

Management in Geneva, in writing. When considering whether to undertake a

feasibility study, FDS will collect and evaluate all pertinent information to

establish working priorities according to activities required in the different

geographical areas.

In order to ensure proper input and knowledge from local sources when

conducting the study (Business Case), FDS will invite Airlines and Agents to

form a CASS Feasibility Study Advisory Group (FSAG). Given their own

particular needs, one group will be comprised of Airlines (FSAG/Airlines) and

another group will be comprised of Agents (FSAG/Agents). All Members

(including Associate Members) serving or having placed stocks of their air

waybills with Agents in the country/area, may provide nominees to the

FSAG/Airline Group.

Once completed, the Feasibility Study Report is submitted to FDS Senior

Management, which decides one of the following courses of action, subject to

prior consultation with the CASS Policy Group (CPG):

(a) Endorsement of the feasibility study;

(b) Endorsement of the feasibility study subject to amplification of certain

details contained therein;

(c) Referral back for additional study to complete the report;

(d) Rejection of the feasibility study.
